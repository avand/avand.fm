Hot Cues

Saved markers in a track that let you instantly jump to a specific point with a button press — the DJ's equivalent of dog-earing the best pages in a book.

A hot cue is a colored, labeled marker placed at a specific moment in a track and stored in your DJ software's library. When you press the assigned pad on your controller or CDJ, playback jumps instantly to that point — no scrubbing, no guessing, no missing the drop.

What DJs use them for

  1. Navigation. Mark the intro, the first drop, the second drop, and the outro. Now you can jump anywhere in the track without touching the jog wheel — useful when you need to preview a section fast or recover from a bad cue.
  2. Performance triggers. Hit a hot cue to re-trigger a vocal chop, slam back to a build, or repeat a phrase on the beat. This turns a static playback button into a live instrument.
  3. Transition markers. Set a cue at your ideal mix-in point and your ideal mix-out point. When the time comes, you're not calculating — you're reacting.

Best practices

  • Land on the grid. Set hot cues on beat grid boundaries so they fire in time. A cue that's a few milliseconds off the beat will sound sloppy every time you trigger it.
  • Color consistently. Pick a system and stick to it — green for safe mix-in points, red for drops, yellow for intros, for example. When you're in a dark booth at 2am, colors communicate faster than labels.
  • Always set a "safe" cue. Pick a point in every track you can always return to mid-set without disaster — a clean intro or a pre-drop moment that gives you time to recover. That's your panic button.

Hot cues vs. loops

A hot cue is a one-shot jump point: press it, and you're there. A loop repeats a defined section until you exit it. They serve different purposes — hot cues are for navigation and triggers; loops are for extending moments or building tension. Many DJs combine them: jump to a hot cue, set a loop running, then exit when the energy is right.

A note on portability

Rekordbox stores hot cues in its library database, not necessarily in the file's ID3 tags. If you hand a USB to another DJ or open a track in different software, your hot cues may not come with it. Export to a rekordbox-formatted USB if you're playing on CDJs — that carries the cue data in a format the players understand. Don't assume your cues are in the file itself.

Crossover

A circuit that splits an audio signal into frequency bands and sends each band to the speaker driver designed to handle it — so the subwoofer only gets bass, and the tweeter only gets highs.

A speaker driver is a physical device, and physics doesn't give it a free pass. A 15-inch woofer cone has way too much mass to vibrate fast enough to reproduce a 10kHz cymbal accurately. A tweeter dome is way too small and fragile to push the air pressure needed for a 60Hz kick drum. The crossover is what keeps each driver in its lane.

What a crossover does

A crossover divides the full audio spectrum at defined "crossover points" — specific frequencies where the signal is split and routed. Everything below the low crossover point goes to the subwoofer. Everything in the middle goes to the mid driver. Everything above the high crossover point goes to the tweeter or compression driver. The result is that each physical speaker only reproduces the frequencies it was built for, which means cleaner, louder, more controlled sound.

Passive vs. active crossovers

There are two types, and they live in very different places:

  • Passive crossover — built inside the speaker cabinet, after the amplifier. The amp drives the full signal into the cabinet, and the crossover filters it internally. Common in home speakers and budget PA gear.
  • Active crossover — a separate processor that splits the signal before amplification, so each frequency band gets its own dedicated amplifier channel. This is what serious club systems use, and for good reason: each amp is optimized for its band, you have precise control over crossover points and levels, and you can tune the system to the room.

When a venue's system tech talks about "tuning the system," they're largely adjusting crossover points, per-band levels, and time alignment between drivers. A well-tuned active crossover system is the difference between bass you feel in your chest and bass that just sounds loud.

Why DJs should know this

In a big club, the system isn't one speaker — it's a network of drivers each handling a slice of the spectrum. Typically: subwoofers handling everything below roughly 80–120Hz, mid-range cabinets above that, and high-frequency drivers on top. When you reach for the bass EQ on your mixer, you are directly controlling what the subwoofer cabinet is doing. When you roll off the highs, you're controlling the tweeters and compression drivers.

This is why bass-heavy tracks hit so differently on a proper club system versus a home setup. At home, your single pair of speakers is trying to do everything. In a club, a subwoofer the size of a refrigerator is doing nothing but the low end, full-time. That's why the floor moves.

It also explains why certain EQ moves that sound subtle in headphones can have dramatic physical effects in a room — you might be adding 3dB to a 60Hz band that's feeding 4 subs stacked in a corner.
